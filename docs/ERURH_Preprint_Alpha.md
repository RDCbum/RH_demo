# ERURH-alpha Preprint (v0)

## 1. Introduction

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) for the completed zeta-like function \(\xi_\alpha\) is recast in the ERURH framework through a collection of finite, auditable certificates. ERURH-alpha introduces bridge invariants \(\log R(s)\) and \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s)\), energy and inertia functionals, and connects them to the zero distribution of the zeta function on the logarithmic scale \(s = \log x\). The core reduction states that if four finite certificates are correct, then RH holds for \(\xi_\alpha\).

This approach separates classical analytic assumptions (explicit formula, zero-free regions, standard RH equivalences) from the new ERU layer, which is expressed as finite rational data plus machine-checkable certificates. A reproducible industrial pipeline regenerates the artifacts, and independent numerical scripts provide exploratory validation. The remainder of this preprint sketches the framework (Section 2), states the master certificate theorem (Section 3), summarizes the pipeline (Section 4), reviews current numerical verification of the bridge (Section 5), and outlines future work (Section 6).

## 2. The ERU framework for primes (alpha case)

We work on the logarithmic scale \(s = \log x\) and define
\[
E(x) = \psi(x) - x,\qquad
\log R(s) = \frac{E(e^s)}{e^s},\qquad
J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s) = \frac{d}{ds}\log R(s).
\]
Here \(\log R(s)\) captures the relative Chebyshev error at scale \(s\), and \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s)\) measures its variation.

The ERU-alpha machinery introduces:
- Global energy and kernel controls to regulate contributions from modes with real part \(\beta > 1/2\).
- Windowed bounds for bridge quantities \(\log R\) and \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) on finite intervals.
- A bridge construction linking prime-scale fluctuations to inertia inequalities.
- A strong-inertia layer that rules out supercritical modes once bridge bounds hold.

These notions are formalized in Lean (see ERURH formalisms and equivalence notes) and are designed to dovetail with the explicit formula on the prime side while remaining finite and auditable on the ERU side.

## 3. Certificates and the master ERURH theorem

ERURH-alpha removes ad hoc ERU axioms by distilling the new content into four finite certificates:
- `GlobalEnergyCertificate_alpha`
- `KernelBlowupCertificate_alpha`
- `BridgeInertiaCertificate_alpha`
- `StrongInertiaCertificate_alpha`

Each certificate has a predicate `Correct`, stated over finite rational inequalities, window bounds, global constants (e.g., \(\kappa\), \(L_{\mathrm{global}}\)), and analytic witnesses (prefactor, lambda, `XiWitnessOn`). The master statement is:

> If the four alpha certificates are correct, then the Riemann Hypothesis holds for \(\xi_\alpha\).

In Lean notation:
```
theorem RH_from_certificates_alpha :
  GlobalEnergyCertificateCorrect_alpha globalEnergyCertificate_true_alpha →
  KernelBlowupCertificateCorrect_alpha kernelBlowupCertificate_true_alpha →
  BridgeInertiaCertificateCorrect_alpha bridgeInertiaCertificate_true_alpha →
  StrongInertiaCertificateCorrect_alpha strongInertiaCertificate_true_alpha →
  RiemannHypothesis xiAlpha
```
This conditional theorem is already formalized: assuming `Correct` for the four certificates, Lean yields RH for \(\xi_\alpha\). Classical analytic assumptions (explicit formula, ERH equivalences) are kept separate from the finite ERU certificates, clarifying dependencies and review scope.

### Why the alpha layer provides a valid route to RH

The ERURH-alpha formalism sets up an exact logical bridge to RH:
- Define the observable \(j_{\mathrm{Rel,\alpha}}(s)\) from \(\psi\) and \(\log R\).
- Postulate strong bounds
  \[
    |J_{\mathrm{rel},\alpha}(s)| \le C_{\mathrm{strong},\alpha}\, e^{-s/2}s^2,
  \]
  coupled with finite certificates (GlobalEnergy, KernelBlowup, BridgeInertia, StrongInertia).
- In Lean we show that if these certificates hold, then RH follows. This is a logical equivalence: **if** the alpha certificate is true in mathematics, **then** RH is true.

This route is non-numerical: the certificates are phrased in terms of rational data, windows, and exact inequalities. The difficulty lies in proving these bounds from analytic number theory; the logical scaffold in Lean is already in place to turn such bounds into a proof of RH once established. Numeric computations (ERURH-beta) cannot prove RH; they only verify behaviour on finite ranges. The alpha layer, however, is formulated at the level of exact bounds and implications and can, in principle, carry a full proof of RH once the analytic estimates are secured.

## 4. Industrial pipeline and reproducibility

The artifacts underlying the certificates are regenerated by a scripted pipeline (see `docs/notes/ERURH_IndustrialPipeline_Alpha.md`):
- `make_rational_bounds.py`: produces rational bounds and constants consumed by the certificates.
- `make_flux_bounds.py`: generates window and tail bounds for bridge quantities.
- `make_certificate_values.py`: assembles certificate parameter values.
- `make_lambda_bounds.py`, `make_prefactor_bounds.py`, and zeta-bound scripts: provide analytic witness bounds (lambda, prefactor, zeta).
- `verify_gate.py`: rebuilds artifacts end-to-end and invokes `lake build ERURH` to ensure Lean proof obligations match the regenerated data.

Outputs include `.lean` files with rational inequalities, JSON/CSV artifacts for bounds, and certificate values. The philosophy is industrial and auditable: every formal lemma is traced back to scripted, documented data generation, enabling external reproduction and review.

## 5. Numerical verification of the bridge (logR and jRel)

### 5.1. Bridge invariants and goals

We study \(\log R(s)\) and \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s)\) on the logarithmic scale. In the ERU bridge:
- \(\log R\) tracks fluctuation amplitude in Chebyshev form.
- \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) encodes inertia: sensitivity to supercritical modes and rate of change.
Formal Lean results yield explicit bounds of the shape \(|\log R(s)|, |J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s)| \le C e^{-s/2} s^2\) when the bridge and strong-inertia certificates hold.

### 5.2. ψ(p^k)-based evaluator (v0.5) for logR

A ψ-based evaluator including prime powers \(p^k\) (primes cached up to \(x_{\max} = 10^7\)) was run on seven windows with meshes of 20 and 100 samples and tolerances \(0.20, 0.10, 0.05, 0.00\). Across 140 and 700 evaluations, no violations of the certified \(\log R\) bounds were observed, providing a robust sanity check for \(\log R\).

### 5.3. jRel audit (Lean vs Python)

Lean treats \(jRel\) as an abstract field of the bridge (`ERUBridge.jRel : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}`) with pointwise bounds on each window/tail: \(jRelBoundWindow \approx 7.886\times 10^{-4}\), \(jRelBoundTail \approx 6.819\times 10^{-3}\) (`FluxWindows.lean`, `FluxBounds.lean`, `InertiaCertificatesAlpha.lean`), with no smoothing or kernels. The Python evaluators use:
- ψ-mode: noisy finite differences of \(\log R(s) = (\psi(e^s) - e^s)/e^s\).
- explicit mode: \(jRel_{\text{main}}(s) = -2\,\Re\big((\rho-1)/\rho \cdot e^{(\rho-1)s}\big)\) over positive-\(\gamma\) zeros up to height \(T\), heuristic tails, no trivial-zero corrections; all diagnostics (smoothing, energy, Chebyshev, etc.) are built on this main term, with smoothing as post-processing only.
Variable/derivative conventions match, but the Python evaluators are approximate, truncated, and heuristic. They are qualitative probes of the intended \(jRel_\alpha\), not certified implementations (see `docs/ERURH_jRel_Audit_Alpha.md`).

### 5.4. Zeros-based explicit evaluator with analytic tails (v2-lite)

We implemented `ExplicitBridgeEvaluatorAlpha` (see `tools/explicit_bridge_evaluator.py`), decomposing
\[
\text{main}(s;T) + \text{tail}(s;T)
\]
with a finite sum over zeros \(|\gamma|\le T\) and an analytic tail bound
\[
E_{\text{tail}}(s,T) = 2 e^{-s/2}\Big[ \frac{\log T}{2\pi} + \frac{(\log T)^2}{4\pi} \Big].
\]
This yields \(|\log R(s)| \le |\text{main}_{\log R}| + E_{\text{tail}}\). For \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) the same L1-style tail is typically too large relative to the tiny certified bounds, so we introduced a refined RMS-style model (see `docs/notes/ERURH_AnalyticTails_Alpha.md`):
\[
E_{\text{tail},J}(s,T) = e^{-s/2} \sqrt{\frac{\log T + 1}{2\pi T}},
\]
heuristically derived from \(J_{\text{tail}}^2 \sim e^{-s} \sum_{|\gamma|>T} 1/\gamma^2 \) and a crude zero-density bound. This refined \(E_{\text{tail},J}\) is much smaller and, in practice, makes the tail contribution negligible compared to the main term in the \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) experiments below, though it remains a v2-lite heuristic rather than a rigorous inequality.

Running the explicit mode with many zeros (e.g., 500 zeros, \(T=1000\), 200 samples per window) yields robust scales for \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\): see the normalization and spectral split below.

### 5.5. Smoothed explicit \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) and scale diagnostics

To probe the typical scale of \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\), we use `tools/numeric_check_bridge_bounds_jRel_smoothed.py` (explicit evaluator, analytic tail):
- uniform mesh per window; evaluate \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}^{\text{main}}\); apply a discrete Gaussian; report \(\max|J_{\mathrm{rel}}|\), RMS, and ratios vs \(jRel\_bound\).
- With 31 zeros, \(T=1000\), 200 samples, \(\sigma=1.5\), radius 5: main-window ratios \(\approx 15\!-\!17\); tail ratio \(\approx 0.5\). Tail \(E_{\text{tail},J}\) is \(\sim 10^{-5}\), so the main truncated sum dominates.
- With 500 zeros (same sampling), the main-window ratios rise to \(\approx 20\!-\!33\), reflecting the larger explicit scale when more zeros are included.

### 5.6. Normalized \(K(s)\) and \(C_{\text{numeric}}\)

Define the normalized diagnostic
\[
K(s) = \frac{e^{s/2}}{s^2}\,|J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s)|.
\]
The certificate bounds have the schematic form \(|J_{\mathrm{rel}}(s)| \le C\,e^{s/2}s^2\), so \(C_{\text{numeric}} = \max K(s)\) measures the effective scale of the explicit evaluator. Using 500 zeros, \(T=1000\), 200 samples/window:
- Main windows: \(K_{\max} \approx 0.35\!-\!0.49\), \(K_{\mathrm{rms}} \approx 0.12\!-\!0.15\); tail window \(K_{\max} \approx 0.0806\).
- Global: \(C_{\text{numeric}} \approx 0.4898\), \(K_{\mathrm{rms,global}} \approx 0.1294\).
This observed \(C_{\text{numeric}}\) for the explicit ζ-model is orders of magnitude above the formal window bounds (\(\sim 7.9\times 10^{-4}\)).

### 5.7. Spectral low/tail split of \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\)

`tools/numeric_jRel_tail_split.py` splits the explicit \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) into
\[
J_{\mathrm{rel}} = J_{\mathrm{low}} + J_{\mathrm{tail}},
\]
with \(J_{\mathrm{low}}\) from the first \(K\) zeros and \(J_{\mathrm{tail}}\) from the remaining zeros up to \(T\), using the same explicit formula. With 500 zeros, \(T=1000\), \(K_{\mathrm{low}}=100\), 200 samples/window:
- Main windows: \(K_{\max}^{\mathrm{low}} \approx 0.17\!-\!0.19\), \(K_{\max}^{\mathrm{tail}} \approx 0.28\!-\!0.46\), \(K_{\max}^{\mathrm{tot}} \approx 0.35\!-\!0.49\); RMS ranges: low \(\approx 0.065\!-\!0.071\), tail \(\approx 0.10\!-\!0.13\), total \(\approx 0.12\!-\!0.15\).
- Tail window: \(K_{\max}^{\mathrm{low}} \approx 0.038\), \(K_{\max}^{\mathrm{tail}} \approx 0.043\), \(K_{\max}^{\mathrm{tot}} \approx 0.081\).
- Global: \(C_{\mathrm{low}} \approx 0.188\), \(C_{\mathrm{tail}} \approx 0.462\), \(C_{\mathrm{tot}} \approx 0.490\).
Mid/high zeros dominate the normalized scale; the observed \(C_{\text{numeric}}\) is driven by the collective tail contribution rather than just the first zeros.

### 5.8. Strong inertia morphism and jRel_strong

The bridgestrong morphism in Lean (`bridge_to_strong` in `InertiaCertificatesAlpha.lean`) is an implication of predicates, not a functional transform \(jRel_{\text{bridge}} \mapsto jRel_{\text{strong}}\). Parameters \(C_{\text{strong}}\) and \(S0_{\text{strong}}\) are set at the certificate level, but Lean does not define a concrete \(jRel_{\text{strong}}(s)\). Our attempt to build a numeric `jRel_strong` (see `tools/eru_strong_inertia_numeric.py`) raises `NotImplementedError` to reflect the absence of a canonical functional map. All numerics reported here refer to the bridge-style explicit \(jRel_{\text{main}}(s)\).

### 5.9. Interpretation

- \(\log R\): ψ(p^k) and the explicit zeros-based evaluator (with analytic tail) agree and show zero violations; certified bounds are comfortably above observed amplitudes.
- \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\): all explicit diagnostics (finite differences, explicit sums with many zeros, smoothing, Chebyshev derivatives, low/tail splitting) reveal a normalized scale \(C_{\text{numeric}} \sim 0.5\) in the main windows, dominated by mid/high zeros, far above the \(10^{-3}\!-\!10^{-4}\) certified bounds. The bridgestrong morphism is logical only; no numeric filter is implied. Current numerics document a robust discrepancy between explicit ζ-dynamics and the certificate scale.
- A serious numerical audit of \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) will require many more zeros (larger \(T\)), sharper tails, possible smoothing/spectral filters, and ideally interval arithmetic and advanced summation. In the present alpha version, \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) is treated as a formal analytic object: numerics are qualitative probes, not verifications.

## 6. Outlook and future work

The ERURH-alpha program has:
- formalized the ERU framework in Lean,
- reduced RH-alpha to four finite certificates,
- built a reproducible industrial pipeline,
- and established an initial numerical layer validating \(\log R\) bounds while highlighting the need for stronger tools for \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\).

Open directions include:
- high-precision numerical verification of \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\): many more zeros (larger \(T\)), stable/FFT-accelerated summation, rigorously justified tails, and interval arithmetic;
- extending ERURH beyond the alpha case (other modes/weights);
- tighter integration between numerical evaluators and formal proofs (e.g., reflecting parts of the explicit formula and tail estimates as Lean lemmas, or using interval certificates inside Lean);
- ERU-style energy/RMS diagnostics for \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\): using integrated or RMS invariants over windows to align numerical checks with the global energy/inertia structure;
- deeper coupling of the analytic and formal layers so future numerical bounds plug directly into certificate predicates;
- reconcile strong-inertia constants with observed \(J_{\mathrm{rel}}\) scales: either refine the analytic model (smoothing/spectral projections), reformulate the certificate (energy/RMS-based), or recalibrate constants (C_strong-type) informed by diagnostics like \(C_{\text{numeric}}\) and the low/tail split.
The architecture of finite certificates plus reproducible pipeline plus formal verification is intended as a template for other analytic problems with heavy computational components.
